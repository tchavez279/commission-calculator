How to Read Your Commission Statement

Commission statements are full of jargon, adjustments, and line items that can be hard to decode. This plain-English guide explains what every section means — and how to spot errors.

📅 March 2025·⏱ 6 min read

Most salespeople glance at the bottom line of their commission statement and move on. That's understandable — the documents are often dense, poorly formatted, and full of terms that aren't explained anywhere. But commission errors are more common than most people realise, and you can't catch them if you don't know what you're looking at.

This guide walks through every section of a typical commission statement so you can verify your pay with confidence.

The Basic Structure of a Commission Statement

While every company formats commission statements differently, most contain the same core sections:

  • Period covered — the date range for which commission is being calculated
  • Deal/transaction list — each qualifying sale with its value and commission amount
  • Gross commission earned — total commission before any deductions
  • Adjustments — clawbacks, draw reconciliation, splits, or corrections
  • Net commission payable — what you actually receive

Understanding Each Line Item

Deal List / Transaction Details

This section shows each deal that generated commission during the period. For each transaction you should see: the deal or account name, the qualifying revenue amount, the commission rate applied, and the resulting commission. Cross-reference this list against your own records — your CRM, deal tracker, or email confirmations. Missing deals are the most common source of commission errors.

Gross Commission

This is the total commission calculated from your deals before any deductions. If your plan is tiered, this section should show how revenue was distributed across tiers. If it shows a single flat calculation, verify that the correct tier rates were applied to the correct revenue brackets.

Adjustments and Deductions

This is the most important section to scrutinise. Common adjustments include:

  • Clawbacks — commission recovered on cancelled or refunded deals. Check that the deal is correctly identified and that the clawback is within the contractual window.
  • Draw reconciliation — if you're on a recoverable draw, any deficit balance is deducted here.
  • Split deductions — your brokerage or team split is calculated and removed.
  • Quota adjustments — some plans adjust commission rates retroactively if quota is revised mid-period. These should be clearly documented.
  • Prior period corrections — adjustments for errors in previous statements. Always verify these match what was communicated to you.

Watch out for: Vague adjustment descriptions like "correction" or "adjustment" with no further detail. You have every right to ask your manager or compensation team for a full explanation of any line item you don't understand.

Accelerator Calculations

If your plan includes quota accelerators, check that the accelerator was applied at the correct threshold and rate. For example, if your accelerator kicks in at 100% quota attainment at 1.5x, verify that the multiplier was applied only to revenue above the quota threshold — not your entire period revenue.

How to Dispute a Commission Error

If you find an error, approach it professionally and with documentation:

  • Gather evidence — signed contracts, CRM records, email confirmations, your comp plan document
  • Calculate the correct amount using our commission calculator so you can present the right figure clearly
  • Email your manager and/or the compensation team with a specific, documented dispute — not a vague complaint
  • Follow up in writing even if the conversation happens verbally
  • Escalate to HR or finance if the issue isn't resolved within one pay cycle

Keep your own records. Don't rely entirely on your company's CRM or reporting. Maintain a personal spreadsheet of every deal you close — value, close date, and expected commission. This makes disputes far easier to resolve.

Verify Your Statement Before Payday

Many companies send commission statements a few days before payroll is processed. Use that window to review the document while there's still time to flag errors before payment. Catching a mistake after the fact means waiting another pay cycle for the correction.
