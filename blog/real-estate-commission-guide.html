Real Estate Commission Guide: How Agent Pay Works

Real estate commission sounds simple — a percentage of the sale price — but splits, brokerage fees, and buyer agent compensation make the actual math more complex. Here's a full breakdown.

📅 April 2025·⏱ 8 min read

Real estate is one of the most commission-driven professions in the world. But the headline number — "agents earn 3% of the sale price" — dramatically oversimplifies how compensation actually works. By the time commissions are split between agents and brokerages, the actual take-home on any given deal can be surprisingly different from what a quick percentage calculation suggests.

This guide explains every layer of real estate commission so you can accurately forecast your earnings on any transaction.

How Real Estate Commission Is Structured

Traditionally, the seller pays a total commission of 5–6% of the sale price, which is then divided between the listing agent's brokerage and the buyer's agent's brokerage. Each agent then splits their portion further with their own brokerage according to their individual split agreement.

Note on recent changes: Following the 2024 NAR settlement, buyer agent compensation is no longer automatically included in the MLS listing. Buyers and their agents now negotiate compensation directly. The traditional 5–6% structure is evolving — confirm current practices in your market.

That $9,625 is before the agent's own business expenses — MLS fees, marketing, E&O insurance, driving, and self-employment taxes. After expenses, net income on this transaction might be closer to $6,000–$7,500.

Understanding Brokerage Splits

Your split with your brokerage is one of the biggest variables in your take-home earnings. Common structures include:

Traditional split

A fixed percentage, such as 70/30 (agent keeps 70%, brokerage takes 30%) or 80/20. New agents often start at 50/50 and negotiate better splits as they prove their production.

Cap-based split

Made famous by Keller Williams, this model has the agent paying a percentage split until they reach an annual cap — say, $20,000 paid to the brokerage. After that, they keep 100% of commission for the rest of the year. High-producing agents benefit enormously from this structure.

100% commission with desk fees

Some brokerages (especially flat-fee and virtual ones) let agents keep 100% of their commission but charge monthly desk fees, transaction fees, or both. This works best for experienced agents with consistent volume.

How Many Deals Do Agents Actually Close?

According to NAR data, the median real estate agent closes 12 transactions per year. However, this figure is heavily skewed by part-time agents. Full-time agents in active markets often close 20–40+ transactions annually, while part-time or newer agents may close just 3–6.
