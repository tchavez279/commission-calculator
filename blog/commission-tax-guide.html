How Commission Income Is Taxed in the US

Commission is taxed as ordinary income — but how it's withheld and estimated is different from a regular salary. Understanding the rules helps you avoid a surprise tax bill.

📅 April 2025·⏱ 7 min read·🧾 Tax & Finance

Disclaimer: This article provides general educational information. It is not tax advice. Consult a qualified CPA or tax professional for guidance specific to your situation.

One of the most common shocks for salespeople in their first big commission month is opening their paycheck and finding far less than expected. The answer is usually taxes — specifically, how supplemental income like commission is withheld by employers.

Is Commission Taxed Differently Than Salary?

At year-end — no. Commission is ordinary income, taxed at your marginal federal rate. Where it differs is in withholding. The IRS classifies commission as "supplemental wages" and allows two withholding methods.

Method 1: Flat Rate (22%)

If commission is paid separately from your salary, employers can withhold a flat 22% federal income tax. This is the most common approach. If your actual marginal rate is 32%, you'll be under-withheld all year and face a large tax bill in April.

Method 2: Aggregate Method

If commission is paid in the same paycheck as your salary, employers must withhold as if the combined amount is one paycheck at your normal rate. This causes a spike in withholding in big commission months but tends to be more accurate over the year.

What this means: If your commission is withheld at 22% but your marginal rate is 32%, set aside an additional 8–12% of every commission payment yourself to avoid a shock at tax time.

Federal Income Tax Brackets (2024)

Commission stacks on top of your salary when determining which bracket applies. Only the income within each bracket is taxed at that rate — the same as how tiered commission works.

FICA: Social Security & Medicare

  • Social Security: 6.2% on wages up to $168,600 (2024). Employer matched.
  • Medicare: 1.45% on all wages, no cap. Employer matched.
  • Additional Medicare: 0.9% on earned income above $200,000 for single filers. Not matched.

Combined, FICA adds roughly 7.65% to your effective tax rate on commission income up to the Social Security wage base.

State Income Tax

Most states also tax commission as ordinary income. Rates vary widely:

  • No state income tax: Texas, Florida, Nevada, Washington, Tennessee, South Dakota, Wyoming, Alaska
  • Low rates (under 5%): Arizona (2.5%), Indiana (3.05%), North Dakota (2.5%)
  • High rates: California (up to 13.3%), New York (up to 10.9%), New Jersey (up to 10.75%)

Self-Employed: Quarterly Estimates

If you receive commission as a 1099 contractor, no tax is withheld automatically. You owe both the employee and employer share of FICA (15.3% self-employment tax) plus income taxes. The IRS requires quarterly estimated payments if you expect to owe more than $1,000 for the year:

  • April 15 — income earned Jan 1 – Mar 31
  • June 17 — income earned Apr 1 – May 31
  • September 16 — income earned Jun 1 – Aug 31
  • January 15 — income earned Sep 1 – Dec 31

Practical tip for 1099 earners: Set aside 25–30% of every commission payment into a dedicated savings account. This covers self-employment tax plus federal and most state income taxes for most earners.

Key Deductions for Commission Earners

  • Home office (self-employed only) — dedicated workspace deduction
  • Vehicle mileage (self-employed) — $0.67/mile for business driving in 2024
  • Health insurance premiums (self-employed) — 100% deductible above the line
  • SEP-IRA contributions — up to 25% of net self-employment income (max $69,000 in 2024)
  • Professional development — courses and subscriptions directly related to your sales role
