SaaS Sales Commission Guide: How Software Reps Get Paid

SaaS commission structures come with their own terminology — ARR, ACV, MRR, clawbacks, accelerators and SPIFFs. This guide explains how software sales compensation works from first principles.

📅 June 2025·⏱ 9 min read

Software sales is one of the most lucrative commission environments in the world — but it also comes with some of the most complex compensation structures. Understanding how your SaaS commission plan actually works is the difference between accurately forecasting your income and being consistently surprised by your paycheck.

Key SaaS Compensation Terms

Before anything else, you need to understand the metrics your commission is based on. SaaS companies typically pay commission on one of the following:

  • ACV (Annual Contract Value) — the total value of a contract normalised to one year. A 3-year $300,000 deal has an ACV of $100,000.
  • ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue) — the recurring revenue component of all active contracts. Commission on ARR typically reflects only the subscription component, not one-time fees.
  • TCV (Total Contract Value) — the full value of the contract including all years. Less common as a commission basis but used in some enterprise environments.
  • MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue) — used more in PLG and SMB-focused companies where deal sizes are smaller and monthly billing is standard.

The most common structure: Most SaaS AEs are paid commission on ACV — the annualised value of new contracts closed. A $100,000 ACV deal at a 10% commission rate pays $10,000 in commission.

How SaaS OTE Is Structured

Most SaaS companies use a 50/50 OTE split for enterprise roles — half base, half variable — though mid-market and SMB roles often trend toward 60/40 or 65/35. The variable component is almost always paid as commission on closed deals rather than a discretionary bonus.

Example: An enterprise AE with a $200,000 OTE, 50/50 split, and a $2,000,000 annual quota would have a $100,000 base salary and earn $100,000 in commission if they close exactly $2,000,000 in ACV. The implied commission rate is 5%.

Quota Structures in SaaS

SaaS quotas are typically annual but measured quarterly or monthly. Common structures include:

  • Annual quota with quarterly ramps — the most common. You have an annual number but performance is tracked quarterly, often with quarterly SPIFFs or bonuses for hitting quarterly sub-targets.
  • Quarterly quota — each quarter is independent. Better for reps in fast-moving markets where pipeline turns over quickly.
  • Ramped quota — new reps start with a reduced quota for their first 1–3 months. Month 1 might be 25% of full quota, month 2 at 50%, month 3 at 75%, full quota from month 4.

SaaS Accelerators

Accelerators in SaaS are usually tiered and apply to revenue above 100% of quota. A typical enterprise AE plan might look like this:

Quota AttainmentCommission Rate
0% – 50%0% (below threshold — no commission)
50% – 100%8% (base rate)
100% – 125%12% (1.5x accelerator)
Above 125%16% (2x accelerator)

Note the cliff at 50% — many SaaS plans include a minimum threshold below which no commission is paid at all. This is a significant downside protection for the employer and a major risk for reps in slow quarters.

Clawbacks in SaaS

SaaS clawbacks are common because customers can cancel subscriptions. If a customer churns within the clawback window — typically 90 to 180 days — the employer can recover the commission paid on that deal. Key points to negotiate:

  • Push for a 90-day clawback window rather than 180 days or longer
  • Negotiate for prorated clawback — if a customer churns after 60 of 90 days, you keep two-thirds of the commission
  • Push for exclusions when churn is outside your control (product failure, company acquired, etc.)
  • Clarify whether clawback applies to base commission only or to accelerator earnings as well

Expansion Revenue and Renewals

How your company handles expansion revenue — upsells and cross-sells to existing customers — varies widely. Some companies pay the full commission rate on expansion deals. Others pay a reduced rate (often 50% of the new business rate). Some pass expansion entirely to account managers or customer success managers and pay nothing to the original AE.

If expansion revenue is in your territory, clarify the rate before signing. A $500,000 account that upsells to $750,000 generates $250,000 in expansion ACV — at even 5%, that's $12,500 in commission you may or may not receive.
