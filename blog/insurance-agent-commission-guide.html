Insurance Agent Commission Guide: How Agents Get Paid

Insurance commission is more complex than most industries — with different rates for different product types, first-year vs. renewal commissions, and override structures. Here's everything you need to know.

📅 May 2025·⏱ 8 min read

Insurance is one of the most commission-driven industries in the world — but it's also one of the most varied. Commission rates differ dramatically between product lines, between captive and independent agents, and between first-year and renewal business. Understanding how your compensation works is essential for forecasting your income and building a sustainable book of business.

Types of Insurance Commission

First-Year Commission

This is the commission paid when a new policy is written for the first time. First-year rates are typically the highest because the agent did the most work — prospecting, quoting, and closing the client. Life insurance first-year commissions can range from 50% to 110% of the first year's premium on some products.

Renewal Commission

Renewal commissions are paid each year the policy remains in force and the client continues paying premiums. These are typically lower than first-year rates — often 2% to 10% — but they accumulate over time and form the backbone of a successful agent's passive income stream.

Override Commission

Overrides are commissions paid to agency owners or managers on the production of agents under them. If you run an insurance agency, you earn an override on every policy your downline agents write, in addition to your own production commission.

Captive vs. Independent Agent Commission

Whether you work as a captive agent (representing one company, like State Farm or Allstate) or an independent agent (representing multiple carriers) significantly affects your commission rates and structure.

  • Captive agents typically receive lower commission rates — often 5% to 10% on property and casualty products — but benefit from company-provided leads, marketing support, training, and sometimes a salary or draw during the ramp period.
  • Independent agents typically earn higher commission rates — often 10% to 15% on P&C and significantly more on life products — but bear their own marketing and lead generation costs.

The residual income potential: A successful independent life insurance agent with 500 active policies paying an average of $1,200/year in premiums earns roughly $84,000 per year in renewal commissions alone — before writing a single new policy.

How to Calculate Insurance Commission

Insurance commission calculation depends on the product type. For premium-based products like life, auto, and home insurance:

  • Annual premium × commission rate = first-year commission
  • Annual premium × renewal rate × years in force = total renewal income

For Medicare Advantage and some health products where CMS sets flat fee caps, calculation is per enrollee rather than percentage-based.

Chargebacks: The Insurance Version of Clawbacks

If a policy lapses or is cancelled within a certain period after being written — typically 9 to 12 months — the carrier can charge back (recover) the first-year commission. This is standard practice and can significantly affect your net income if your lapse rate is high.

Strategies to minimise chargebacks include thorough needs analysis before writing a policy, setting clear expectations with clients about premium payment, and following up proactively when payments are missed.
