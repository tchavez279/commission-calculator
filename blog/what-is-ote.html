What Is OTE in Sales? On-Target Earnings Explained

OTE is one of the most commonly used — and misunderstood — terms in sales compensation. Here's exactly what it means, how it's structured, and how to evaluate it when comparing job offers.

📅 June 2025·⏱ 6 min read

If you've ever looked at a sales job posting, you've seen it: "$80,000 base + $40,000 commission = $120,000 OTE." On-Target Earnings is the headline number recruiters and hiring managers use to describe how much you'll make — but it comes with a big catch that most candidates overlook.

What Does OTE Mean?

OTE stands for On-Target Earnings. It represents the total compensation — base salary plus commission — you would earn if you hit exactly 100% of your assigned sales quota. It is not a guaranteed number. It is a target.

The formula is simple:

OTE = Base Salary + (Commission Rate × Quota)

For example, if your base salary is $60,000 and your quota is $500,000 at a 10% commission rate, your OTE is $60,000 + $50,000 = $110,000. But you only earn that $50,000 in commission if you actually close $500,000 in sales.

Why OTE Can Be Misleading

The problem with OTE as a headline number is that it assumes 100% quota attainment — which, at many companies, the majority of reps never achieve. If only 50% of the sales team hits quota, the average rep is earning far less than the advertised OTE.

This is why the most important question you can ask in any sales interview is: "What percentage of your reps hit quota last year?" If the answer is below 60–65%, the OTE figure is largely theoretical.

What Is a Good OTE Split?

The ratio of base salary to variable commission within OTE matters a lot. A 50/50 split means half your pay is at risk — high upside but also high risk. A 70/30 split is more conservative and better suited for longer sales cycles or newer reps still building their pipeline.

OTE Above and Below Quota

OTE is defined at exactly 100% quota — but what happens above and below that mark is just as important to understand before you accept an offer.

Below quota

If you hit 80% of quota, your commission earnings drop proportionally. On a $500,000 quota at 10%, hitting $400,000 earns you $40,000 in commission instead of $50,000. Your total earnings would be $60,000 base + $40,000 = $100,000 — $10,000 below OTE.

Above quota — accelerators

Many plans include accelerators that increase your commission rate once you exceed 100% of quota. This is where OTE can significantly understate your earning potential if you're a strong performer. A rep who hits 130% of quota with a 1.5x accelerator above 100% can earn substantially more than the advertised OTE.

How to Evaluate OTE When Comparing Offers

  • Ask about quota attainment rates. What % of reps hit OTE last year? What was the median rep's actual earnings?
  • Model realistic scenarios. Use our commission calculator to see what you'd earn at 70%, 100%, and 120% of quota — not just the rosy 100% case.
  • Compare the base salaries separately. Two offers with the same OTE but different base/variable splits have very different risk profiles.
  • Check the quota history. Has the quota increased significantly year over year? Rising quotas can erode OTE even if your rate stays the same.
  • Understand accelerators. Uncapped accelerators above quota can make a lower OTE offer more valuable than a higher OTE with a cap.

OTE in Different Industries

OTE structures vary significantly across industries. In SaaS and tech sales, OTE is almost universally the standard way to present comp. In real estate and insurance, where reps are often independent contractors, the concept of OTE is less formal — earnings are purely commission-based with no base component. In retail, base wages typically dominate with commission as a smaller add-on, making OTE a less meaningful metric.
