How to Negotiate Your Sales Commission Structure

Most salespeople accept the first compensation plan they're offered. The ones who earn significantly more have learned to negotiate — not just on base salary, but on the commission structure itself. Here's how.

📅 June 2025 · ⏱ 8 min read · 💼 Negotiation

When you're offered a sales job, the conversation usually goes like this: the hiring manager presents you with a comp plan, explains the OTE, and waits for you to say yes. What most candidates don't realise is that almost every element of that plan is negotiable — the base salary, the commission rate, the quota, the accelerators, and even the clawback policy.

This guide will walk you through exactly how to approach that negotiation, what to ask for, and what to watch out for. Whether you're accepting a new role or reviewing your current plan at the start of a new fiscal year, these principles apply.

Quick tip: Before any negotiation, run your numbers in our commission calculator. Knowing exactly what different rates and quotas translate to in annual income gives you a powerful anchor in the conversation.

1. Understand the Full Structure Before You Negotiate Anything

The single biggest mistake salespeople make is negotiating on commission rate alone. A 12% rate sounds better than a 10% rate — but if the quota is 40% higher, you may actually earn less. Before you push on any single number, map out the whole plan:

  • Base salary — What is the guaranteed floor?
  • On-Target Earnings (OTE) — What do you earn at exactly 100% of quota?
  • Quota — What is the revenue target, and how was it set?
  • Commission rate — Is it flat, tiered, or based on gross margin?
  • Accelerators — Does the rate increase above quota? At what threshold?
  • Clawback policy — Can earned commission be taken back if a deal falls through?
  • Payment timing — Is commission paid on signed contract, invoice, or cash received?

Once you have the full picture, you can model your realistic earnings under different scenarios — not just the rosy "if you hit 100% of quota" version the company presents.

2. Research What the Market Actually Pays

You can't negotiate effectively without knowing what's normal. Commission rates vary enormously by industry, so do the research before the conversation:

  • Check Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, RepVue, and LinkedIn Salary for comp data at similar companies.
  • Talk to people in the same role at other companies — sales communities on Reddit and Slack are excellent for this.
  • Look at the company's own job postings for similar roles. If they list OTE ranges, you know their ceiling.
  • Ask recruiters what the market rate is — it's literally their job to know, and most will tell you.

Coming in with a specific external data point ("I've seen AEs at similar-stage SaaS companies earning $X OTE at this quota level") is far more effective than a vague "I was hoping for more."

3. Focus on OTE and Quota Attainability, Not Just Rate

A company can offer you a 15% commission rate and still lowball you if the quota is set at a level where hitting it is nearly impossible. Before accepting any plan, ask the uncomfortable question:

The most important question to ask: "What percentage of your sales reps hit quota last year?" If the answer is below 60%, the quota is likely too aggressive — or the product is too hard to sell. Either way, your expected earnings are lower than the OTE suggests.

Ideally, you want to be joining a team where 65–80% of reps hit quota. That means the target is achievable, the product is working, and the OTE is a realistic number rather than a fantasy.

If quota attainment is low, negotiate the quota itself down, or push for a higher base to compensate for the variable income risk. A 60/40 base-to-commission split is reasonable in most B2B sales roles; pushing toward 65/35 or 70/30 if the quota is aggressive is entirely fair.

4. Push Hard for Accelerators

Accelerators are the single biggest lever for top earners. If you're confident in your ability to exceed quota, an accelerator clause can transform your income. Here's how to negotiate them:

Know what to ask for

A standard accelerator kicks in at 100% quota attainment and pays out at 1.5x the normal rate. The best plans have multiple tiers — for example, 1.5x from 100–125%, then 2x above 125%. Push for the multiple tiers if you can.

Negotiate the threshold

If the company won't budge on the base commission rate, ask if they'll lower the threshold at which the accelerator kicks in — say, 85% of quota instead of 100%. This protects your upside even in a slightly below-target month.

Make sure it's uncapped

Some plans cap total commission earnings. This is a serious red flag for anyone who expects to outperform. Always ask: "Is there any cap on commission earnings?" and get the answer in writing. Uncapped commission is a significant benefit — worth taking a slightly lower base to secure.

5. Challenge the Clawback Policy

Clawbacks allow a company to recover commission if a customer cancels, doesn't pay, or a deal is reversed. This is standard practice — but the terms matter enormously.

  • A 30-day clawback window is fair and common.
  • A 90-day window is typical in SaaS and insurance.
  • A 180-day or 12-month clawback is aggressive — push back.
  • Full clawback (100% of commission) vs. prorated clawback (based on time elapsed) makes a big difference. Negotiate for the latter.

Also clarify: does the clawback apply if the customer is churned for reasons outside your control (e.g., company goes bankrupt, product failure)? If so, push for an exception clause.

6. Get Everything in Writing

This should go without saying, but verbal agreements on commission are essentially worthless. Before you start, ensure you have a signed compensation agreement that covers:

  • The exact commission rate and any tier breakpoints
  • The quota amount and how it will be measured
  • Accelerator thresholds and multipliers
  • The clawback policy, in full
  • When commission is paid (and what triggers payment)
  • What happens to earned but unpaid commission if you leave the company

If the company is hesitant to put any of these in writing, treat that as a serious warning sign. Legitimate employers are happy to document what they've agreed to.

The Bottom Line

Negotiating your commission structure requires preparation, market knowledge, and the confidence to ask for specifics. Most hiring managers expect candidates to negotiate — and a thoughtful counter-proposal based on data is rarely going to cost you the offer.
