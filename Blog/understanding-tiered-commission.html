Understanding Tiered Commission Structures

Tiered commission can dramatically increase your earnings if you understand how the brackets work — or leave you badly underpaid if you don't. Here's everything you need to know, with real examples.

📅 May 2025·⏱ 6 min read·📊 Education

If your compensation plan includes tiered commission, you're in one of the most powerful — and most misunderstood — pay structures in sales. Done right, tiers reward your best months disproportionately. Done poorly, they can quietly suppress your earnings while looking generous on paper.

This article explains exactly how tiered commission works, walks through worked examples, highlights the most common misunderstandings, and shows you how to use our calculator to model your own plan accurately.

What Is Tiered Commission?

Tiered commission (also called progressive commission) is a structure where different commission rates apply to different portions of your sales revenue. As you sell more, you unlock higher rates — but crucially, those higher rates only apply to the revenue within each bracket, not your total sales.

This is identical in concept to how income tax brackets work. Earning more never reduces your take-home pay, because the higher rate only ever applies to the additional revenue above each threshold.

The most important thing to understand: In a tiered structure, higher rates apply only to the portion of revenue within each bracket — not to your total sales. Crossing a tier boundary never reduces your total commission.

A Worked Example

Let's say your plan has three tiers:

TierRevenue RangeCommission Rate
Tier 1$0 – $50,0005%
Tier 2$50,001 – $100,0008%
Tier 3Above $100,00012%

Now suppose you close $130,000 in revenue this month. Here's how your commission is calculated:

PortionRevenueRateCommission
First $50,000$50,0005%$2,500
Next $50,000$50,0008%$4,000
Remaining $30,000$30,00012%$3,600
Total$130,000—$10,100

Your effective commission rate is $10,100 ÷ $130,000 = 7.77% — even though your top-tier rate is 12%. This is normal and expected: the 12% rate only applies to the $30,000 above the $100,000 threshold.

The Common Misconception That Costs Reps Money

Many salespeople incorrectly believe that crossing a tier threshold applies the new rate to all their revenue, not just the portion above the threshold. Under this mistaken reading, our example rep would calculate: $130,000 × 12% = $15,600. That's a $5,500 overestimate.

This matters most when you're close to a tier boundary. Suppose you're at $98,000 for the month and have a $5,000 deal on the table. Knowing that only $2,000 of the commission on that deal is at the 12% rate (the $3,000 below the boundary earns 8%) helps you accurately forecast your month — rather than getting a nasty surprise when the paycheck arrives.

Flat vs. Retroactive Tiered Plans

Not all tiered plans are progressive. Some companies use a retroactive or threshold model, where crossing a tier applies the new rate to all your revenue — including what you already sold. This model is much rarer (and much more lucrative for reps), so confirm which type you have.

How Tiers Interact With Quotas and Accelerators

Many plans layer tiered rates on top of quota-based accelerators. For example, your normal plan might be tiered (5% / 8% / 12%), and then once you hit 100% of your quota, an accelerator kicks in that multiplies your commission rate by 1.5x across all future revenue for the remainder of the period.

These hybrid structures are common in enterprise SaaS and financial services, and they can be extraordinarily lucrative for top performers. They also make accurate commission calculation almost impossible to do by hand. This is exactly what our Advanced mode was built for.

Red Flags in Tiered Plans

Not all tiered plans are structured in your favour. Here are warning signs to watch for:

  • Tier resets that aren't calendar-based: If tiers reset per deal rather than per month or quarter, you rarely accumulate enough in a single transaction to reach the higher tiers.
  • Overly wide first brackets: If Tier 1 runs from $0 to $200,000 at 3%, and your average monthly revenue is $180,000, the higher tiers are essentially decorative.
  • No clarity on reset periods: When do the tiers reset? Monthly? Quarterly? Annually? A quarterly reset might work in your favour in a strong quarter — or against you in a slow one.
  • Team-based tiers: Some plans tier based on team performance rather than individual. Your commission rate then depends on your colleagues' results, which you can't control.

How to Calculate Tiered Commission Accurately

For a quick calculation, our commission calculator handles tiered structures automatically in Advanced mode. Enter your revenue, set up your tier brackets and rates, and the breakdown shows you exactly how much each tier contributes to your total commission.

For manual calculation:

  • Sort your tiers from lowest to highest threshold.
  • For each tier, calculate the revenue that falls within that bracket (not your total revenue).
  • Multiply each bracket's revenue by its rate.
  • Sum all the tier payouts for your total commission.
