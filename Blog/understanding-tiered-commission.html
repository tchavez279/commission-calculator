Understanding Tiered Commission Structures

Tiered commission can dramatically increase your earnings if you understand how the brackets work — or leave you badly underpaid if you don't. Here's everything you need to know, with real examples.

📅 May 2025·⏱ 6 min read·📊 Education

If your compensation plan includes tiered commission, you're in one of the most powerful — and most misunderstood — pay structures in sales. Done right, tiers reward your best months disproportionately. Done poorly, they can quietly suppress your earnings while looking generous on paper.

The most important thing to understand: In a tiered structure, higher rates apply only to the portion of revenue within each bracket — not to your total sales. Crossing a tier boundary never reduces your total commission.

A Worked Example

Let's say your plan has three tiers:

TierRevenue RangeCommission Rate
Tier 1$0 – $50,0005%
Tier 2$50,001 – $100,0008%
Tier 3Above $100,00012%

If you close $130,000 in revenue this month, here's how your commission breaks down:

PortionRevenueRateCommission
First $50,000$50,0005%$2,500
Next $50,000$50,0008%$4,000
Remaining $30,000$30,00012%$3,600
Total$130,000—$10,100

Your effective commission rate is 7.77% — even though your top-tier rate is 12%. That's normal: the 12% only applies to the $30,000 above the $100,000 threshold.

The Common Misconception

Many salespeople incorrectly believe that crossing a tier threshold applies the new rate to all their revenue. Under this mistaken reading, the example above would yield $130,000 × 12% = $15,600. That's a $5,500 overestimate — a shock when the paycheck arrives.

Flat vs. Retroactive Tiered Plans

Not all tiered plans are progressive. Some companies use a retroactive model, where crossing a tier applies the new rate to all your revenue — including what you already sold. This model is much rarer and far more lucrative, so always confirm which type you have.

TypeHow It WorksCommon In
Progressive (standard)Higher rate applies only to revenue within that bracketMost B2B, SaaS, insurance
RetroactiveCrossing a tier applies the new rate to all revenueSome retail, real estate
Flat acceleratorRate changes only after hitting quotaMost quota-based plans

Red Flags in Tiered Plans

  • Tier resets that aren't calendar-based: If tiers reset per deal, you rarely accumulate enough in a single transaction to reach the higher tiers.
  • Overly wide first brackets: If Tier 1 runs from $0 to $200,000 at 3% and your average monthly revenue is $180,000, the higher tiers are essentially decorative.
  • No clarity on reset periods: When do the tiers reset — monthly, quarterly, annually? Always ask.
  • Team-based tiers: Some plans tier based on team performance. Your commission then depends on your colleagues' results, which you can't control.
