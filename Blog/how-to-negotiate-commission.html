How to Negotiate Your Sales Commission Structure

Most salespeople accept the first compensation plan they're offered. The ones who earn significantly more have learned to negotiate — not just on base salary, but on the commission structure itself.

📅 June 2025·⏱ 8 min read·💼 Negotiation

When you're offered a sales job, the conversation usually goes like this: the hiring manager presents you with a comp plan, explains the OTE, and waits for you to say yes. What most candidates don't realise is that almost every element of that plan is negotiable — the base salary, the commission rate, the quota, the accelerators, and even the clawback policy.

This guide will walk you through exactly how to approach that negotiation, what to ask for, and what to watch out for. Whether you're accepting a new role or reviewing your current plan at the start of a new fiscal year, these principles apply.

Quick tip: Before any negotiation, run your numbers in our commission calculator. Knowing exactly what different rates and quotas translate to in annual income gives you a powerful anchor in the conversation.

1. Understand the Full Structure Before You Negotiate Anything

The single biggest mistake salespeople make is negotiating on commission rate alone. A 12% rate sounds better than a 10% rate — but if the quota is 40% higher, you may actually earn less. Before you push on any single number, map out the whole plan:

  • Base salary — What is the guaranteed floor?
  • On-Target Earnings (OTE) — What do you earn at exactly 100% of quota?
  • Quota — What is the revenue target, and how was it set?
  • Commission rate — Is it flat, tiered, or based on gross margin?
  • Accelerators — Does the rate increase above quota? At what threshold?
  • Clawback policy — Can earned commission be taken back if a deal falls through?
  • Payment timing — Is commission paid on signed contract, invoice, or cash received?

2. Research What the Market Actually Pays

You can't negotiate effectively without knowing what's normal. Commission rates vary enormously by industry, so do the research before the conversation:

  • Check Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, RepVue, and LinkedIn Salary for comp data at similar companies.
  • Talk to people in the same role at other companies — sales communities on Reddit and Slack are excellent for this.
  • Look at the company's own job postings for similar roles. If they list OTE ranges, you know their ceiling.
  • Ask recruiters what the market rate is — it's literally their job to know, and most will tell you.

3. Focus on OTE and Quota Attainability

A company can offer you a 15% commission rate and still lowball you if the quota is set at a level where hitting it is nearly impossible. Before accepting any plan, ask the uncomfortable question:

The most important question to ask: "What percentage of your sales reps hit quota last year?" If the answer is below 60%, the quota is likely too aggressive. Your expected earnings are lower than the OTE suggests.

Ideally, you want to be joining a team where 65–80% of reps hit quota. If quota attainment is low, negotiate the quota itself down, or push for a higher base to compensate for the variable income risk.

4. Push Hard for Accelerators

Accelerators are the single biggest lever for top earners. A standard accelerator kicks in at 100% quota attainment and pays out at 1.5x the normal rate. Push for multiple tiers — 1.5x from 100–125%, then 2x above 125%. If the company won't budge on the base rate, ask if they'll lower the threshold at which the accelerator kicks in. And always confirm: is commission uncapped? A cap on total earnings is a significant red flag for anyone who expects to outperform.

5. Challenge the Clawback Policy

Clawbacks allow a company to recover commission if a customer cancels or a deal reverses. Terms matter enormously:

  • A 30-day clawback window is fair. A 90-day window is typical in SaaS. A 180-day or 12-month window is aggressive — push back.
  • Negotiate for prorated clawback (based on time elapsed) rather than full clawback (100% of commission).
  • Push for an exception clause if the churn is outside your control (e.g. company bankruptcy, product failure).

6. Get Everything in Writing

Before you start, ensure you have a signed compensation agreement covering the exact commission rate and tier breakpoints, quota amount, accelerator thresholds and multipliers, the full clawback policy, payment timing, and what happens to earned but unpaid commission if you leave. If the company is hesitant to document any of this, treat that as a serious warning sign.
