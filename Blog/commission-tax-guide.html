How Commission Income Is Taxed in the US

Commission is taxed as ordinary income — but the way it's withheld, reported, and estimated is different from a regular salary. Understanding the rules helps you avoid a surprise tax bill and keep more of what you earn.

📅 April 2025·⏱ 7 min read·🧾 Tax & Finance

Disclaimer: This article provides general educational information about how commission income is typically taxed in the US. It is not tax advice. Tax situations vary — consult a qualified CPA or tax professional for guidance specific to your circumstances.

One of the most common shocks for salespeople in their first big commission month is opening their paycheck and finding far less than they expected. "Where did my money go?" The answer is usually taxes — specifically, the way supplemental income like commission is withheld by employers.

Understanding the rules means you can plan your finances accurately, avoid underpaying the IRS, and make smart decisions about when to defer income and how to use deductions. Here's a clear, practical guide.

Is Commission Taxed Differently Than Salary?

In terms of what you ultimately owe the IRS at year-end — no. Commission income is classified as ordinary income and taxed at your marginal federal income tax rate, just like your salary.

Where commission is different is in how it is withheld by your employer throughout the year. The IRS classifies commission as "supplemental wages" and allows employers to withhold federal income tax using one of two methods.

The Two Withholding Methods

Method 1: The Flat Rate Method (22%)

If your commission is paid separately from your regular salary (as a distinct payment), your employer can withhold a flat 22% federal income tax. This is the most common approach because it's simple to administer.

The 22% flat rate is fine if your marginal tax rate is around 22% — but if you're a high earner in the 32% or 37% bracket, you'll be under-withheld all year and face a large tax bill in April. Conversely, if you're in the 12% bracket, you're over-paying throughout the year.

Method 2: The Aggregate Method

If your commission is paid together with your regular salary in the same paycheck, your employer must use the aggregate method — calculating withholding as if the combined amount is a single paycheck at your normal withholding rate. This tends to result in more accurate withholding but can cause a spike in taxes in months with large commissions.

What this means for you: If your commission is withheld at 22% but your actual marginal rate is 32%, you're building up a tax deficit all year. Set aside an additional 8–12% of every commission payment yourself to avoid a shock in April.

Your commission income stacks on top of your salary when determining which bracket applies. Here are the 2024 federal brackets for single filers:

Remember: these are marginal rates. Only the income within each bracket is taxed at that rate. If you earn $120,000 total (salary + commission), you don't pay 24% on all of it — only on the portion above $100,525.

FICA Taxes: Social Security and Medicare

In addition to federal income tax, commission income is also subject to FICA taxes:

  • Social Security tax: 6.2% on wages up to $168,600 (2024 limit). Your employer matches this.
  • Medicare tax: 1.45% on all wages, no cap. Again, employer matched.
  • Additional Medicare tax: 0.9% on earned income above $200,000 for single filers. This is not matched by employers.

Combined, FICA adds roughly 7.65% to your tax burden on commission income (up to the Social Security wage base).

State Income Tax

Most states also tax commission income as ordinary income at the state level. Rates vary significantly:

  • No state income tax: Texas, Florida, Nevada, Washington, Tennessee, South Dakota, Wyoming, Alaska, New Hampshire (on earned income)
  • Low rates (under 5%): Arizona (2.5%), North Dakota (2.5%), Indiana (3.05%)
  • High rates: California (up to 13.3%), New York (up to 10.9%), New Jersey (up to 10.75%)

If you live in a high-tax state and earn significant commission, state tax can add another 8–13% to your overall tax burden — making effective total tax rates on commission income of 45–50% possible for high earners in California or New York.

Self-Employed Commission Earners: Quarterly Estimates

If you receive commission as an independent contractor (receiving a 1099 rather than a W-2), no tax is withheld for you automatically. You're responsible for paying both the employee and employer share of FICA (15.3% combined, known as self-employment tax) plus federal and state income taxes.

The IRS requires self-employed people to pay quarterly estimated taxes if they expect to owe more than $1,000 for the year. The deadlines are:

  • April 15 — for income earned January 1 to March 31
  • June 17 — for income earned April 1 to May 31
  • September 16 — for income earned June 1 to August 31
  • January 15 — for income earned September 1 to December 31

Missing these payments results in underpayment penalties, even if you pay the full amount owed at tax time.

Practical tip for 1099 earners: Set aside 25–30% of every commission payment into a dedicated savings account. This covers self-employment tax plus federal and most state income taxes for the majority of earners. Adjust up if you're in a high-tax state or high income bracket.

Deductions Commission Earners Can Claim

If you're a W-2 employee, your ability to deduct unreimbursed work expenses was largely eliminated by the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act. However, there are still meaningful deductions available:

  • Home office deduction (self-employed only) — if you work from a dedicated space in your home
  • Vehicle mileage (self-employed) — $0.67 per mile for business driving in 2024
  • Health insurance premiums (self-employed) — 100% deductible above the line
  • Retirement contributions — SEP-IRA contributions of up to 25% of net self-employment income (max $69,000 in 2024) are fully deductible
  • Professional development — courses, books, and subscriptions directly related to your sales role (self-employed)
